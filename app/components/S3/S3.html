How taking time out can help you improve your wellnes

  • Research has demonstrated that certain people such as some:
    1. senior managers in the private and public sectors
    2. individuals in the helping professions (including medical and ancillary professionals)
    3. individuals characterised by a sense of urgency and time-pressures
    4. fail to draw a clear line between their working attitudes and their ‘style of living’ outside work.
  • Some individuals, who display both a high level of job commitment, and competitive working styles, may also have a tendency to follow the same strategies in their leisure-time; that is, they are highly combative and competitive.
  • This type of behaviour is known as the ‘spill-over phenomenon’.
  • If you display ‘spill-over phenomenon’ behaviour there is an increased likelihood that you will, in the medium to long term feel:
    1. a lower psychological and physical health, as well as
    2. a general dissatisfaction with living.
  • There is evidence that when such people are able to adopt a recreational style which compensates for their hard-driven working styles by embracing pursuits which may be quite the reverse and are less:
    1. time pressurised and exacting
    2. competitive
    3. measurements linked to targets and goals
  • They become more relaxed and mellow in character, they are also able to:
    1. use time more sparingly / selectively, and
    2. can be engaged in sitting around apparently ‘doing nothing’ without experiencing frustration or guilt.
  • What is often not recognised enough by organisations and people is that taking ‘time out’ is a skilled art!
  • It is beneficial to your wellness, particularly if you are a person who feels time pressurised, to take ‘time out’ and indeed say:
    1. “I am busy taking time out”
    2. “I am busy relaxing”.
